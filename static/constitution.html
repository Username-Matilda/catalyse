Holacracy Constitution Reference

Note: This is a quick reference. For the complete constitution, visit holacracy.org/constitution

Article 1: Organizational Structure

1.1 Roles

A Role is an organizational building block defined by:

  • Purpose: The role's reason for existing
  • Domains: Exclusive authority over specific resources
  • Accountabilities: Ongoing activities to perform

1.1.2 Domains

An asset, process, or resource that a Role has exclusive authority to control on behalf of the Organization.

Examples: Security policies, compute resource allocation, fermentation protocols

1.1.3 Accountabilities

An ongoing activity that a Role is expected to perform for the Organization.

Examples: Monitoring security incidents, coordinating responses, tracking metrics

Article 2: Circle Structure

2.1 Circles

A Circle is a Role that contains other Roles to organize work in a specific domain.

2.1.3 Policies

Policies are grants or constraints of authority that apply across a Circle's Roles.

Examples:

  • "Compute allocations over $10k require Research Lead approval"
  • "All fermentation experiments must log temperature daily"
  • "Governance facilitation rotates weekly among circle members"

Article 3: Governance Process

3.1 Purpose

Governance meetings process tensions into structural changes—updates to roles, policies, and circle structure.

3.2 Governance Meeting Process

Each tension is processed through these phases:

  1. Present Proposal: Proposer describes structural change
  2. Clarifying Questions: Information-seeking only, no reactions
  3. Reaction Round: Each person shares perspective, no discussion
  4. Amend & Clarify: Proposer may modify based on reactions
  5. Objection Round: Test for harm or backwards movement
  6. Integration: If objections exist, work together to integrate them

3.2.5 Valid Objections

An objection is valid if the proposal would:

  • Cause harm to the organization
  • Move the organization backwards
  • Create a new tension worse than the one being addressed

Invalid objections:

  • "I have a better idea" → Offer after adoption
  • "I don't like it" → This is a reaction, not harm
  • "We tried this before" → Past attempts don't predict future outcomes

Article 4: Tactical Meetings

4.1 Purpose

Tactical meetings synchronize team work and address operational tensions—not structural changes.

4.2 Tactical Meeting Process

  1. Check-in: Brief personal sharing
  2. Checklist Review: Recurring operational items
  3. Metrics: Key numbers that inform decisions
  4. Project Updates: Progress on multi-step initiatives
  5. Tension Processing: Triage operational gaps, identify next actions
  6. Closing Round: Reflection or appreciation
Key Distinction: Tactical meetings coordinate work. Governance meetings change structure. If a tension requires changing roles/accountabilities/domains, save it for governance.
